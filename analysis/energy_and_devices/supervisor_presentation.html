Study scope

Two linked datasets, same 20 facilities

  • Energy systems database (Teufen thesis) Solar, grid, hybrid systems at Malawian health facilities

  • Medical devices database (Häussner thesis) Device inventory, functionality, funding & maintenance

Both datasets are linkable via facility_id, enabling the first cross-validated empirical analysis of the energy–healthcare quality link in this sample.

Cross-Dataset Overview

Do energy system outcomes and device outcomes move together?

Energy vs. device functionality

Interpretation — Facilities with a higher share of functional energy systems also tend to have more functional medical devices. Each point is one facility; size encodes the total number of devices surveyed. The positive slope supports the hypothesis that energy quality is a structural precondition for device functionality.

Funder fragmentation: energy vs. devices

Interpretation — Facilities fragmented in energy funding tend also to be fragmented in device funding. This suggests that funding fragmentation is a facility-level structural trait, not specific to one asset type. Colour encodes device functionality: more fragmented facilities tend to show lower device functionality.

Investment timing: energy age vs. device delivery

Interpretation — Points above the diagonal received medical devices after their energy infrastructure was installed; points below the line received devices first. The majority of facilities fall above the diagonal, consistent with a pattern where energy investment precedes device delivery — yet device functionality still varies considerably, suggesting that energy investment alone does not guarantee sustained device operability.

Cross-dataset correlations (summary)

Three Spearman tests across the two datasets:

Variable pair ρ p
Energy functionality ~ Device functionality positive < 0.05
N energy funders ~ N device funders positive < 0.05
Mean energy install year ~ Device delivery year positive < 0.05

All three cross-dataset associations are statistically significant and directionally consistent.

What this means

  • Energy and device outcomes are not independent — they co-vary at facility level
  • Funder fragmentation is a systemic, cross-sectoral governance problem
  • Investment timelines are loosely coordinated but energy infrastructure tends to arrive first

These correlations motivate a joint analysis of energy quality and healthcare service readiness.

Donor Governance

Does having shared funders across energy and devices improve outcomes?

Per-facility funder profile

Interpretation — Each bar shows how many funders are active only in energy (blue), only in devices (orange), or in both (green). Facilities at the bottom of the chart have the most integrated donor engagement. Most facilities show little funder overlap — donors tend to fund either energy or devices, rarely both — suggesting siloed project planning is the norm.

Co-funding vs. preventive maintenance

Interpretation — Number of shared funders (x-axis) vs. facility self-reported preventive maintenance practice (y-axis, from “Never” to “Routinely”). The trend line tests whether integrated co-funding corresponds to better maintenance culture. A positive association here would suggest that donors who coordinate across asset types also promote or require better maintenance practices.

Funding alignment (Jaccard similarity)

Interpretation — Jaccard similarity measures the overlap between a facility’s energy funders and device funders (intersection / union). Left: higher Jaccard → lower device non-functionality; Right: higher Jaccard → better preventive maintenance. Together, these panels suggest that funding coherence (same donors across asset types) is associated with better outcomes on both dimensions.

Funder Fragmentation

Does having many different energy funders lead to worse system functionality?

Funder fragmentation bar chart

Interpretation — Facilities ordered by Simpson funder diversity (most fragmented at top). The breadth of the legend — up to 26 distinct funding organisations — is itself a key finding. The DHO must theoretically maintain familiarity with 26 different donors’ preferred equipment specifications and spare-parts chains. Facility S (bottom, single funder) has 0% non-functionality; Facility E (top) has among the highest.

Fragmentation scatter plots

Interpretation — Top: Simpson funder diversity vs. proportion non-functional; Bottom: number of distinct funders vs. non-functionality (more x-axis spread, more legible). Facility J sits well above the trend line (~65% non-functional); Facility S anchors the lower-left (1 funder, 0% non-functional). Regional pattern (North/South) does not explain the trend.

Statistical results: fragmentation vs. non-functionality

Spearman correlations (n = 20)

Measure ρ p
Simpson funder diversity 0.686 0.001
Number of distinct funders 0.685 0.001
Simpson manufacturer diversity 0.659 0.002

All three operationalisations of fragmentation yield statistically robust results (p < 0.01, survives Bonferroni correction).

Robustness checks

Sensitivity (excl. Facility J, n = 19)

Measure ρ p
Simpson funder diversity 0.647 0.004
Number of distinct funders 0.659 0.003

→ The finding is not an artefact of a single outlier

Age-adjusted partial correlation

Measure partial r p
Simpson funder diversity 0.703 0.001
Number of distinct funders 0.681 0.001

→ Fragmentation predicts non-functionality independently of system age

Key takeaway: fragmentation

Important

Main finding — Funder fragmentation is a significant, robust, and age-independent predictor of energy system non-functionality in Malawian health facilities (ρ ≈ 0.69, p = 0.001; partial r ≈ 0.70 after age adjustment).

Roughly 47% of the rank variance in non-functionality is explained by funder fragmentation alone (ρ²), which is substantial for n = 20.

Caveats for reporting:

  • n = 20 → results are exploratory and hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory
  • Facility J remains an influential point warranting qualitative investigation
  • Manufacturer diversity adds little beyond funder count (near-ceiling clustering) and may be dropped from the final figure

Maintenance Effectiveness

Does preventive maintenance predict energy system and device functionality?

Energy systems: maintenance vs. functionality

Four sub-analyses (energy systems)

  1. PM frequency vs. % functional (facility-level, coloured by mean system age) — Does routine maintenance correlate with higher functionality rates?

  2. PM vs. functionality (system-level, coloured by individual system age) — Finer-grained view: does each maintained system last longer?

  3. Maintenance party vs. functionality (stacked bar) — Are DHO-maintained vs. donor-maintained systems more functional?

  4. System longevity by PM level & status (age distribution) — Do routinely maintained systems have older functional systems on average?

Research question

The energy dashboard distinguishes between:

  • maintenance_by: who is nominally responsible for maintenance (DHO vs. donor)
  • pm_score: actual reported preventive maintenance frequency (never / sometimes / routinely)

Key tension: In many facilities, the DHO is responsible for maintaining donor-installed systems — but without spare parts budgets or technical training from those donors. This analysis quantifies whether that accountability gap translates into measurable functionality differences.

Medical devices: maintenance vs. functionality

Four parallel sub-analyses (medical devices)

  1. PM frequency vs. % functional devices (facility-level, coloured by mean device age)

  2. PM vs. functionality (device-level, coloured by device age)

  3. Maintenance party vs. functionality (stacked bar by device category) — Planned: add facet to existing figure (Fig. 3.15)

  4. Device longevity by PM level & status — Are routinely maintained devices older on average (i.e., do they last longer)?

Why this matters

The medical device dataset distinguishes:

  • Donor-funded, DHO-maintained assets → the most common and most problematic category
  • Government-funded, DHO-maintained assets → baseline accountability

Planned extension: For every device category (cold chain, diagnostics, therapeutic, ICT), compute the share that is donor-funded but government-maintained, and compare across categories to identify where the accountability gap is most acute.
